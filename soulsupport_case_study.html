01 — Overview

The problem we set out to solve

Despite the global recognition of mental health as critical to overall well-being, accessible and personalized care remains elusive for the majority. We asked: what does a digital system look like that genuinely meets people where they are?

The gap

High costs, professional shortages, long wait times, and geographic barriers leave millions without the mental health support they need.

🔍

The shortfall

Existing apps like Headspace, Calm, and Mindspa each cover only a slice of user needs — none offer a truly comprehensive, AI-driven, therapist-connected platform.

✦

Our answer

SoulSupport — a holistic mobile system combining AI assistance, real therapist booking, mood tracking, guided exercises, and mental health resources in one place.

Design goals

  • Make mental health support geographically and financially accessible to underserved users
  • Deliver AI-personalized plans based on individual wellbeing assessments
  • Connect patients directly with verified, bookable therapists
  • Reduce stigma through private journaling, a compassionate chatbot, and community features
  • Protect sensitive user data with encryption and GDPR/HIPAA-aligned practices
02 — Research & Discovery

Understanding the user before writing a single pixel

We conducted a mixed-method research phase — a professional interview with a licensed psychotherapist and a quantitative questionnaire targeting a diverse demographic.

Expert interview insights

"Offline sessions are more effective for diagnosis — body language, facial expressions, and environmental tools are irreplaceable. But online sessions are a breakthrough in accessibility. Reach patients anywhere in the world, at any time."

— Dr. Mohamed Shokrey AboelSadat, Cognitive Behavioral Therapist

The interview surfaced three critical design principles: transparent pricing and scheduling, lightweight pre-session tests that supplement (not replace) real interaction, and exercises that must be tailored — never one-size-fits-all.

Survey findings — 46 respondents

Communication preference

73.9% of respondents prefer face-to-face discussions, but 26.1% are comfortable going fully online — reinforcing the need for a hybrid model.

Top mental health challenges

Anxiety, stress, and depression dominated responses. Overthinking, burnout, and insomnia were notable secondary mentions across multiple age groups.

Barriers to app use

Privacy concerns led the list, followed by cost, perceived usefulness, and technical friction. Trust is the foundational currency of this product.

Highest-valued features

Online documentation (95.5%), mood tracking (93.5%), mental health tests (89.1%), and daily exercise plans (89.1%) — all validated by strong supermajorities.

Wireframe-to-hi-fi rationale — Home screen

The home screen was designed around a single principle: the most important action must require the fewest taps. The SoulMate chatbot button dominates the top third — a deliberate break from conventional app grids that bury the most critical feature in navigation. The supporting feature grid uses soft color-coding rather than icons alone, as research showed that abstract icons slow comprehension for first-time users.

06 — Testing & Iteration

What users told us when we got out of the way

We ran usability sessions focusing on the four core flows — onboarding, mood logging, chatbot interaction, and therapist booking. Here's what we learned and how we responded.

Key findings

⚡

Onboarding friction

Users were confused about the post-signup "test screen" — many thought the assessment was mandatory and felt pressured, not welcomed.

⚡

Chatbot cold start

When SoulMate opened, the blank chat state caused hesitation. Users didn't know what the AI could and couldn't do, creating anxiety rather than relief.

⚡

Therapist trust signals

Users wanted to see more before booking — credentials, session format (video/audio), and estimated wait time were frequently requested.

◈

Mood emoji resonance

The animated emoji in mood logging had the highest delight score across all sessions. Users felt it "actually understood" them — lean into this.

◈

Plan page underused

The personalized plan was buried. Users who discovered it loved it, but most missed it entirely on first sessions — a discoverability failure.

✓

Test skippability

Adding "Skip for now" to the initial assessment dramatically improved onboarding completion rate in the second round of testing.

Key design iterations

Before

Mandatory post-signup assessment with no skip option. Users felt evaluated immediately, creating anxiety at the point of greatest vulnerability.
→

After

Assessment framed as "a quick check-in to personalize your experience" with a prominent "Skip for now" option. Completion improved; anxiety signals reduced.

Before

SoulMate opened to a blank chat screen. Users stared at a blinking cursor with no indication of what to type or what the AI could help with.
→

After

Chatbot opens with a warm greeting plus pre-filled prompt chips ("I feel...", "I'm worried about...", "Curious why...") — reducing cognitive load at the moment of distress.

Before

Therapist cards showed only name, specialty, and a "Book" button. Users reported not trusting professionals they knew nothing about.
→

After

Therapist profiles expanded to include verified credentials badge, session count, rating with review count, available formats (video/audio/chat), and a bio section — replicating the trust cues of a clinic visit.

Before

"My Program" was a tab in the bottom navigation. Most users never discovered it during first sessions; some thought the app didn't have personalized content.
→

After

Personalized plan surfaced as a card on the home screen with a progress indicator — "Today's Plan: 2 of 4 activities complete." Engagement with plan content tripled in subsequent testing.

Designer's reflection

This project taught me that designing for mental health is fundamentally an act of trust architecture. Every decision — from the warmth of a color to the phrasing of an error message — either builds or erodes the user's willingness to be vulnerable.

The most significant tension we navigated was between clinical credibility and emotional warmth. Too sterile and users feel surveilled. Too casual and they don't trust the medical validity. Finding that middle path — a product that feels like a conversation with a wise, caring friend who also happens to be a professional — is the core design challenge of mental health UX.

If I were to extend this project, the next frontier is crisis detection — using AI to identify linguistic patterns in chat that signal acute distress, and surfacing emergency resources proactively rather than waiting for a user to ask.

What I'd do differently

  • Recruit participants with lived mental health experience earlier — student demographics alone don't capture the full user spectrum
  • Prototype the AI chatbot interaction earlier in the process — its emotional register requires more testing cycles than static UI
  • Co-design sessions with a licensed therapist as a regular design partner, not just an interview subject
  • Conduct accessibility audits from the start — mental health users frequently include people with anxiety, ADHD, or dyslexia who are underrepresented in standard UX research
